Westminster Calling

The Policy Desk/18 May 2026

Leadership challenge on the horizon for Starmer after local election disaster.

The crisis

Britain might once have been renowned for political stability, but those days are long gone. Contrasting with the country’s first Prime Minister Robert Walpole, who served for almost 21 years in the 18th century, the UK has had six Prime Ministers in just the past decade. The way things are looking right now, London is on course for a seventh.

For all his successes on the international stage, UK Prime Minister Sir Keir Starmer has proven unpopular domestically. Labour has suffered, dropping from 39 per cent in polls at the 2024 election to 18 per cent today. But Starmer’s personal approval rating sits at −46 per cent, down from 20.5 per cent two years ago. Politicians might take polls with a pinch of salt, but this month has displayed public displeasure with Labour. May’s local elections were always going to hurt the party, but few appreciated just how disastrous they would be. Labour lost 1,500 councillors and 38 councils across England, and it was annihilated in Wales. Former strongholds like Sunderland flipped to Reform, whilst in London, the party lost Lewisham and Hackney to the Green Party. Even the beleaguered Conservatives managed to poach Westminster back.

Some argue that the local elections were effectively a referendum on Starmer’s leadership, so he has shouldered much of the blame. Labour MPs are increasingly concluding that a change of leader will improve their fortunes, and at the time of writing, 91 lawmakers have urged him to resign. Labour MP Catherine West even went so far as to call for Cabinet members to challenge Starmer’s leadership, threatening she would run against him as a “stalking horse” otherwise. The PM hoped to use the days after the vote for a fresh start, but his post-weekend “reset” speech, alongside Wednesday’s King’s Speech, were far overshadowed by leadership speculation.

Hanging on

Despite escalating calls for his departure, Starmer has insisted that he will not throw in the towel, emphasising his intentions to see out his five-year mandate in full. The PM’s allies argue that getting on with the day job is far more important, and having blamed constant leadership changes under the Conservatives for many of Britain’s current woes, Labour would be unwise to emulate such antics. Moreover, as the Iran war sends energy prices and inflation skyward, many believe that the country needs a steady hand to manage the crisis. These arguments make sense, but with Starmer having lost the support of so many in his party, it looks like it will be difficult for him to continue until 2029. He might not be going gently, but that good night is likely coming.

The contenders

With Starmer’s days looking numbered, the obvious next question is who might replace him. No one has launched a formal leadership challenge yet, but among the main contenders are former Health Secretary Wes Streeting as well as Manchester Mayor Andy Burnham, respectively considered to be on the right and left flanks of the party. Labour Party rules stipulate that the leader needs to be a Member of Parliament, so Burnham would first need to be elected to the Commons before receiving the support of 81 MPs to run for the leadership.

This, of course, limits Burnham in launching immediate action and would prima facie bolster Streeting’s odds. Yet the Ilford North MP is conscious that being seen as the one who toppled Starmer could weaken his chances. Indeed, Streeting has said that he hopes that Burnham will make it to the leadership challenge. It is therefore a stalemate right now, but Labour MP Josh Simons’s triggering a by-election on Thursday moves things one step forward. The election for the Greater Manchester seat of Makerfield is expected to be held on 18 June.

The move was explicitly designed to get Burnham into Parliament, and having been allowed to run for selection as the party’s candidate, the self-styled “King of the North” looks to be on his way to Westminster. Burnham is popular locally, but considering Labour’s local election performance, his victory is hardly a foregone conclusion. Reform UK has pledged to throw the kitchen sink at Makerfield, and should it win, Burnham’s electoral gambit will look spectacularly ill-judged.

Policy platforms and market reaction

Regarding their policy platforms, both sides have made some overtures towards Brussels. On Saturday, Streeting told reporters that he believed Brexit to have been a “catastrophic mistake” and that he hoped to rejoin the EU. Burnham initially expressed hopes that the UK would rejoin but backtracked after criticism from other Labour lawmakers who are weary of reopening the Brexit debate — especially considering how such sentiment might go down in leave-voting Makerfield.

Markets are also conscious that a leftward lurch after Starmer’s departure could see the government weaken its commitment to fiscal rules, and Burnham’s comments last year that London should not be “in hock to the bond markets” do not exactly inspire confidence. Unsurprisingly, then, amid mounting likelihood that Burnham will throw his hat into the ring, 10-year bond yields rose 0.19 per cent on Friday to 5.18 per cent — their highest since 2008.

He might not be going gently, but that good night is likely coming. Affinity Research

Things are even more drastic for 30-year bond yields, which rose 0.2 per cent to their highest since 1998. Britain already faces the highest borrowing costs in the G7, so adding fuel to the fire by jettisoning spending restraints could spell disaster. At the same time, sterling fell 0.6 per cent against the dollar to USD 1.332, marking a monthly low and extending an 0.9 per cent fall on Thursday. Now, Burnham has tempered these earlier sentiments, telling ITV News at the weekend that he supported the UK’s current fiscal rules and that the UK needed a plan to get debt under control. It will take more than that for markets to feel fully comfortable, though!

What it means

Outlook

Protracted leadership uncertainty at the worst possible time. Starmer might be hanging on for now, and he has pledged to fight any leadership challenge which comes his way. While businesses and consumers might not be happy with parts of his programme, changing leadership could make things worse. As the Iran war’s economic consequences become ever more apparent, protracted uncertainty about who is in charge might look indulgent. Inflation and high energy costs will hit everyone, but without proper leadership to guide the UK through the crisis, it could well be harder hit than most. Is that a price worth paying?

No End in Sight

The Oil Desk/18 May 2026

Tensions rise in the Middle East, boosting oil prices; and Trump’s unremarkable trip to Beijing nevertheless assuages fears of a growing rift.

Stalling negotiations

Negotiations between the US and Iran via Pakistan are continuing this week, but there are no suggestions that they have been successful. The issue of enrichment and uranium materials is still a roadblock, as neither country seems willing to compromise on this point.

There are reports that the US and Israel are conducting the most intense preparations for further attacks on Iran since the current ceasefire was agreed last month, and there have already been some more attacks. On 17 May, the UAE’s only nuclear power plant was hit by a drone. The strike at the Barakah plant started a fire at an electrical generator within the compound.

Later on Sunday, Saudi Arabia said that it had destroyed three drones entering its airspace from Iraq, and Riyadh spokesman Major General Al-Maliki said that his defence ministry “reserves the right to respond at the appropriate time and place”. Iraq has previously launched drones at other Gulf countries, including Kuwait, despite the ceasefire.

Oil prices rose early on Monday in response to the attacks and continued stalling. Brent rose by 2.03 per cent to USD 111.50 per barrel, and WTI jumped by 2.59 per cent to USD 108.20 per barrel. At the time of writing, the two benchmarks sit at USD 110.00 per barrel and USD 106.20 per barrel, respectively.

Trump in Beijing

There had been some optimism leading up to US President Donald Trump’s visit to China last week, because it had been announced that one of the many topics to be discussed was the war in the Middle East. As the conflict has dragged on, China has become more vocal about its wish for peace, and many analysts had therefore hoped that Chinese President Xi Jinping would be able to push Trump towards some sort of deal — at the very least a method to increase flows through the Strait of Hormuz — while he was in Beijing.

For Iran, the Clock is Ticking, and they better get moving, FAST, or there won’t be anything left of them. TIME IS OF THE ESSENCE! Donald Trump, on social media

But this was not to be, and after returning to Washington, Trump hurled further threats at Iran on social media. The message is by no means the first of its kind, but the renewed intimidation does nothing to indicate a change of attitude.

The trade angle

The trip to China was notable for another reason. In Trump’s tariff blitz last year, China was the hardest hit; at this point last year, Beijing faced 145 per cent tariffs and had retaliated with its own. A truce was established in October, but it was not definitive, and it was expected that Trump and Xi would talk about the trade barriers on last week’s visit.

But, despite referencing trade agreements broadly, Trump told reporters on Air Force One that the two leaders had not discussed tariffs at all. Instead, he said, they had agreed to establish a “Board of Trade” to manage the economic relationship without having to resume tariff negotiations, but no details were provided.

On Saturday, China’s commerce ministry offered a somewhat contradictory account. It issued a statement saying that Washington and Beijing had come to a preliminary agreement to reduce some tariffs, also confirming some agricultural and aircraft deals which Trump had already touted.

What it means

A ceiling on trade-war risk has been restored — for now. Whatever the details are, the high tariffs which caused oil prices to sink last year seem less likely to reemerge than they did previously. After Trump’s trip, a China expert from the Center for Strategic and International Studies said of US-China relations that “compared to where we were a year ago… we’ve had a counterrevolution and we’re back at stability”. If the situation remains like this, one possible limit to future spikes in oil prices has been neutralised. And the Gas desk reports on the downstream LNG implications of Trump’s “everything energy” claims.

The Great Bauxite Squeeze

The Dry Cargo Desk/18 May 2026

Guinea risks, weaker Chinese demand, and Middle East disruption are shaking the bauxite trade and Capesize markets.

The bauxite trade

Global bauxite trade is an important component of dry bulk shipping, particularly for long-distance routes linking West Africa and Asia. In recent years, growth in seaborne flows has been supported mainly by rising exports from Guinea to China, which have underpinned strong demand for Capesize vessels due to the long voyage distances involved.

However, the outlook for the market is becoming more uncertain. Structural factors such as changing supply conditions in Guinea, evolving Chinese demand for aluminium, and persistently weak pricing are all beginning to reshape expectations for future trade flows. At the same time, geopolitical instability and disruptions along key shipping routes are adding further volatility to logistics and freight markets. As a result, the bauxite trade is entering a more complex phase in which policy decisions, demand constraints and supply chain fragility are increasingly influencing both volumes and shipping dynamics.

Guinea’s exports to China more than doubled between 2020 and 2025, reaching around 178 Mn T and underpinning strong Capesize demand due to the roughly 100-day round voyage time on the West Africa–China route.

Export caps and oversupply

Despite this growth, the market is increasingly challenged by oversupply, weaker aluminium demand and geopolitical instability. Guinea, the world’s largest bauxite exporter, is considering export restrictions in 2026 to stabilise prices and protect smaller miners whose margins have been squeezed by falling prices. A proposed export cap of 150 Mn T would represent a reduction of about 15 per cent from 2025 levels and could release around 46 Capesize vessels back into the market, equivalent to nearly 80 per cent of the Capesize fleet scheduled for delivery in 2026. Such a move would likely place significant downward pressure on freight rates, particularly if implemented during the second half of the year.

However, some market participants believe a 150 Mn T cap may prove too restrictive. Guinea’s bauxite exports rose by 25 per cent year-on-year in the first quarter of 2026 to 60.9 Mn T, driven largely by sustained Chinese demand. Chinese-linked producers dominated shipments, with Société Minière de Boké exporting 18 Mn T and Chalco increasing volumes by 35 per cent to 8 Mn T. Other key contributors included AGB2A/SDM, CBG and AMC. Given the strong start to the year, many analysts see a more realistic export range of 170–190 Mn T for 2026.

Chinese aluminium production has now reached the country’s official ceiling of 44.2 Mn T, limiting long-term growth in bauxite demand. At the same time, bauxite prices have fallen to around USD 32–38 per T, their lowest levels since 2022, increasing pressure on Guinean authorities to intervene. Beyond export caps, the government is also considering tax reforms designed to encourage investment in rail infrastructure, ports and domestic refining capacity.

Strait of Hormuz disruption

The market is also being disrupted by instability around the Strait of Hormuz, which has forced Gulf aluminium producers to adopt costly and complex emergency logistics solutions. With direct shipments of alumina and bauxite disrupted, cargoes are being transhipped through Indian anchorages onto smaller vessels for delivery to the UAE before being transported overland to smelters. While the system has allowed operations to continue, it remains inefficient, expensive and highly vulnerable to weather conditions, particularly during the Indian monsoon season when handling bauxite and alumina becomes significantly more difficult.

Outlook

A triple squeeze is reshaping the market. Together, the prospect of Guinean export controls, slowing Chinese aluminium growth and Middle Eastern supply chain disruption is creating significant uncertainty for both global aluminium markets and dry bulk shipping. The second half of 2026 will be decisive.

“Everything Energy”

The Gas Desk/18 May 2026

After his two-day visit to Beijing, Trump claims China wants to buy energy from the US.

A new phase

Global LNG markets are entering a new phase of rebalancing, marked by shifting trade flows, geopolitical disruption and evolving demand patterns across major importing regions. Europe is preparing for a structural pivot toward US LNG, while developments in China suggest a possible reopening of one of the most important demand channels in the global gas system. At the same time, supply disruptions in the Middle East continue to underpin elevated prices and reshape how buyers approach energy security.

Europe’s reliance on US LNG is set to increase sharply in the coming years as the continent seeks to reduce dependency on Middle Eastern and Russian volumes. According to the Institute for Energy Economics and Financial Analysis (IEEFA), Europe’s dependence on US LNG imports is projected to surge by around 80 per cent by 2028, as ongoing disruptions in traditional supply routes force a reorientation of trade flows. This shift reflects both necessity and policy, as Europe attempts to secure reliable supply amid instability in the Persian Gulf and an ongoing effort to phase out Russian energy. US LNG has emerged as the most flexible large-scale alternative, but the transition also raises questions around pricing, infrastructure readiness and long-term contract structures.

China’s “insatiable appetite”

At the same time, developments in Asia are adding a new layer of complexity to global LNG dynamics. Comments from US President Donald Trump following discussions with Chinese President Xi Jinping suggest that China may be open to resuming imports of US energy, including LNG, after a prolonged freeze. Trump said that China wants to buy “everything energy” from the US, highlighting the country’s “insatiable appetite” for fuel. A revival of LNG trade between the two nations would reconnect the world’s largest LNG buyer with the largest LNG exporter, potentially reshaping flows across the Atlantic and Pacific basins.

China wants to buy “everything energy” from the US. Donald Trump, following discussions with Xi Jinping

However, significant barriers remain. China continues to apply a 25 per cent tariff on US LNG, which has been the primary obstacle to resumed trade following earlier tariff disputes. Even without a formal agreement, analysts note that China could unilaterally reduce or remove the tariff if it requires additional volumes, especially as buyers prepare for peak summer demand. Ship tracking data already suggests some tentative movement, with two US LNG cargoes loading from Sabine Pass and Plaquemines in Louisiana signalling Tianjin as their potential destination. If completed, these would represent the first US LNG deliveries to China since February 2025.

Despite this potential reopening, China’s demand profile remains relatively weak in the near term. S&P Global notes that China has been importing less than its full contracted volumes and has largely managed the supply disruption by diverting cargoes and staying out of the spot market for much of the year. This strategy has helped mitigate the immediate impact of the Middle East crisis on Asian markets, even as other buyers have struggled. China accounted for about 28 per cent of Qatar’s LNG exports in 2025, underscoring its central role in global demand. Chinese firms also hold around 21 Mn T per year of US LNG term contracts, of which 5.4 Mn T are already being delivered, while another 8.7 Mn T are scheduled to begin delivery between July and October.

Tight market conditions

Meanwhile, market conditions remain tight. Global LNG prices continue to reflect supply disruptions tied to the conflict in the Middle East, particularly around the Strait of Hormuz, which has historically carried about a fifth of global LNG flows. Platts assessed the June JKM benchmark price for Northeast Asia at USD 18.465 per MmBtu on 15 May, representing a 3 per cent increase from the previous assessment and about 73 per cent higher than prewar levels. These elevated prices have kept pressure on importing countries, particularly in Asia, where demand is more sensitive to price fluctuations.

Affinity view

The LNG market is fragmenting along geopolitical lines. Europe is locking in greater volumes from the US as it reshapes its supply base, while Asia, led by China, is balancing between demand restraint and selective re-entry into the market. The potential revival of US-China LNG trade could provide support for American project developers, particularly if it leads to new long-term contracting activity. Before the trade dispute, the US was the fifth-largest LNG supplier to China, exporting about 4.2 Mn T in 2024, accounting for more than 5 per cent of total Chinese imports. As the Oil desk reports, the broader US-China trade relationship also saw movement last week.

Taking Advantage of Geopolitics

The Containers Desk/18 May 2026

Spot rates continue to jump as carriers press their advantage, but dark clouds are gathering on the horizon.

Rate surge

There remains little prospect of an imminent end to the war in the Middle East. The US continues to push Iran to the negotiating table, but Iran believes that it is “winning”, as the US electorate feel an increasingly acute pinch from rising energy and consumer prices.

But as long as the conflict continues, liner companies will continue to push freight rates higher. Last week, spot rates posted their highest weekly gains in months, after carriers implemented both higher emergency surcharges and capacity cuts. The Drewry World Container Index leapt 12 per cent to USD 2,553 per FEU, with the strongest increases posted on the Far East–US routes.

On these routes, liner companies have implemented both Emergency Fuel Surcharges (EFS) and Peak Season Surcharges (PSS), passing on higher operating costs onto shippers. On Shanghai–New York, spot rates jumped 14 per cent on the week to USD 4,252 per FEU, while Shanghai–Los Angeles rose 10 per cent to USD 3,357 per FEU. Carriers continue to tighten capacity to support the freight market, with Drewry reporting seven blank sailings scheduled for this week. Moreover, others continue to push the rate higher, with Yang Ming Marine Transport announcing a General Rate Increase (GRI) of USD 2,000 per FEU effective 15 May. Drewry expects further GRI announcements this week.

Rates on Asia–Europe routes also strengthened, also driven by carriers pushing through rate hikes. Rates on Shanghai–Genoa jumped 20 per cent to USD 3,701 per FEU, while Shanghai–Rotterdam climbed 11 per cent to USD 2,413 per FEU. Higher Freight All Kinds (FAK) rates led the charge, driven by carriers tightening capacity.

Bypass routes and congestion

The Asia–Europe peak season typically begins in the latter half of Q2, as shippers begin building inventories ahead of the end-of-year holiday season. But just as last year, when they front-loaded imports to counter US President Trump’s tariffs, shippers appear to be moving earlier this year because of tightening tonnage and uncertainty generated by supply chain disruptions because of the war in the Middle East.

Speaking of which, carriers have continued to ship volumes to the Middle East, utilising trucking to bypass the closed Strait of Hormuz. Trucking routes linking Red Sea and Gulf of Oman ports, such as Yanbu and Fujairah, to some of the largest ports in the Arabian Gulf, such as Jebel Ali and Basra, have opened up. And carriers are charging record-high freight rates, rising beyond even those at the height of the pandemic with the release of unprecedented pent-up demand. Congestion at these ports, however, has soared, further tightening tonnage and supporting the wider containership market’s fundamentals.

By contrast, volumes handled at US ports continued to drop in April. At the Port of Long Beach, handled cargo volumes fell 5.7 per cent on the year last month, falling to 817,992 TEU. However, early last year, the port handled record volumes on the sharp front-loading and, despite the drop in volumes in March, Long Beach became the US’s busiest port through Q1 this year.

Outlook

Uncertainty and volatility borne out of the conflict in the Middle East continue to support container imports and, according to Xeneta, the market is in a unique position in which both shippers and carriers are “happy” with freight levels, particularly on Transpacific trades. Although rates have risen well above pre-conflict levels, they have “remained effectively flat” over the past month, according to Xeneta’s Peter Sand. A factor behind the plateau is US shippers delaying signing new long-term contracts because of the uncertainty emanating from the Middle East, wary of locking in rates at higher levels than necessary, depending on when and how the conflict is resolved.

Risk

Short-term support masks longer-term vulnerability. The impact of the conflict is becoming increasingly wide-ranging. More and more economic indicators are being published that underline the extent to which the global economy is being affected. Inflation is creeping higher and the latest Chinese economic data was weaker than expected. Accompanying the data, Beijing warned of the “severe” global economic environment that has been caused by the war. So, while there may be short-term support for the freight market, longer-term — should the current status quo be maintained — weakness is expected as the conflict takes its toll.

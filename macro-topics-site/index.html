The Policy Desk/[DD Month YYYY]

A single sentence that frames the piece. Two clauses, separated by a semicolon or em-dash; this is what the reader sees first, after the headline.

The opening

The opening paragraph carries a drop-cap. Keep it short to medium length — one to two ideas, scene-set the piece, do not bury the lede. The drop cap can be toggled off, small, or large in the reading settings, so do not write around it.

The second paragraph carries the reader from the scene-set into the substance. Cite a figure or a name if it earns its place. Where a claim is provisional or worth flagging for follow-up — a wire report not yet confirmed, a figure pending revision — add an inline reference like this one,1 which links to the “Items to confirm” block at the foot of the piece.

Sub-section heading

Sub-headings break a piece into legible chunks. Three to five sub-sections is the right shape for a roughly 600–900 word piece. Each sub-section can carry its own share anchor (the § appears on hover at the right edge), so a reader can send a colleague the exact subsection that matters to them.

Use plain text for figures: “Brent closed Wednesday at $108.36 a barrel, up 2.9 per cent on the week.” A numeric callout that earns more weight should go in an analysis block (below) rather than a chart — this is a text-only template.

What it means

The takeaway, in one paragraph. The analysis block carries the editorial judgement that the prose has been building toward. One paragraph is the right shape: a bold lead-in for the headline judgement, then the supporting reasoning in plain text. Use this where you would otherwise be tempted to draw a chart.

Cross-references to other pieces are written like this: the Oil desk explains why the headline number does not yet show in the freight curve. The xref machinery turns the link into a labelled jump and surfaces a “Return to” pill so the reader can come back without losing place.

A second paragraph bridges the scene-set to the first data component — the prices table. Brief context frames the numbers so they read as a narrative, not a spreadsheet.
